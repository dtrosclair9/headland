# US Sugarcane Domain Reference

This document is internal product / engineering reference for **Headland**, a field-mapping and records SaaS for US sugarcane growers. It captures the specific vocabulary, varieties, mills, crop-cycle dates, and pest/disease names that real Louisiana and Florida growers use in 2025/2026, so the product (variety dropdowns, region landing pages, scouting categories, records UI, marketing copy) doesn't read like generic "ag tech."

Last updated: 2026-05-07

> **Scope note.** Texas closed its last raw-sugar mill (Rio Grande Valley Sugar Growers / Santa Rosa) in 2024, and Hawaii's last commercial cane harvest was 2016 (HC&S, Maui). For all practical purposes, US mainland cane production today = **Louisiana + Florida**, in roughly that order by acreage but with Florida winning on yield per acre.

## 1. Geography

### Louisiana

Louisiana sugarcane is grown across **25 parishes** on roughly **530,000 to 540,000 acres** (USDA FSA reported just over 536,000 acres in 2024; NASS reported 520,000 harvested for sugar and seed). The state ground a record **17.36 million tons** of cane in the 2024 grinding season.

The "cane belt" runs along Bayou Teche, the lower Mississippi River, and Bayou Lafourche. Core parishes (rough order of historical acreage):

- **Iberia** (New Iberia, Jeanerette) — Cajun Sugar Co-op, St. Mary Sugar Co-op
- **St. Mary** (Franklin, Patoutville) — Sterling Sugars, Enterprise Factory (M.A. Patout & Son)
- **Assumption** (Belle Rose, Paincourtville) — Lula Sugar Factory, Westfield Sugar Factory
- **Lafourche** (Thibodaux, Raceland) — Lafourche Sugars, Raceland Raw Sugar
- **Iberville** (White Castle) — Cora-Texas Manufacturing
- **St. Martin** (St. Martinville) — Louisiana Sugar Cane Co-op (LASUCA)
- **Terrebonne** (Houma) — historically large; Houma is also home to the USDA-ARS Sugarcane Research Unit
- **Pointe Coupee** (Lakeland) — Alma Plantation
- **Vermilion, Acadia, St. James, St. John the Baptist, Ascension, West Baton Rouge** — significant additional acreage; expansion has pushed the belt north toward Baton Rouge as cane displaces traditional row crops

**Operating raw-sugar mills (factories) in Louisiana, 2025/2026 — eleven total:**

1. **Alma Plantation** — Lakeland (Pointe Coupee)
2. **Cajun Sugar Co-op** — New Iberia (Iberia)
3. **Cora-Texas Manufacturing Co.** — White Castle (Iberville)
4. **Enterprise Factory (M.A. Patout & Son)** — Patoutville (St. Mary) — oldest continuously operating sugar mill in the US (founded 1825)
5. **Lafourche Sugars** — Thibodaux (Lafourche)
6. **Louisiana Sugar Cane Co-op (LASUCA)** — St. Martinville (St. Martin)
7. **Lula Sugar Factory (Lula-Westfield, LLC)** — Belle Rose (Assumption)
8. **Raceland Raw Sugar** — Raceland (Lafourche) — owned by M.A. Patout since 1996; ~15,000 tons/day grinding capacity
9. **St. Mary Sugar Co-op** — Jeanerette (Iberia)
10. **Sterling Sugars** — Franklin (St. Mary) — owned by M.A. Patout since 1994; ~12,750 tons/day average
11. **Westfield Sugar Factory** — Paincourtville (Assumption)

**Refining:** **Louisiana Sugar Refining (LSR)** in Gramercy (St. James Parish) is the major refinery, jointly owned by Sugar Growers and Refiners of Louisiana (SUGAR) and Imperial Sugar.

### Florida

Florida sugarcane is concentrated in the **Everglades Agricultural Area (EAA)** — roughly 700,000 acres of drained peat/muck soils south of Lake Okeechobee. Cane occupies **~417,000–440,000 harvested acres** (NASS 2024: 417,000 harvested at 42.1 tons/acre; ~17.6 million tons produced). About **71% on muck (organic) soils and 29% on sand (mineral) soils** — sand acreage has been growing as citrus retreats from greening (HLB) disease.

**Counties:**

- **Palm Beach** (Belle Glade, Pahokee, South Bay, Bryant, Canal Point) — by far the largest acreage; Florida Crystals' Okeelanta and Osceola mills, SCGC's Bryant Sugar House
- **Hendry** (Clewiston) — US Sugar's headquarters and Clewiston Mill / "Sugarland"
- **Glades** (Moore Haven) — US Sugar acreage
- **Martin** (Indiantown) — northern edge of cane production
- **Okeechobee** — limited acreage on the lake's north shore

**Operating mills in Florida (2025/2026) — three:**

1. **US Sugar — Clewiston Mill** (Hendry) — the largest single sugar mill in the United States. US Sugar farms ~230,000 acres across Hendry, Glades, Martin, and Palm Beach. (The historical second US Sugar mill, Bryant, was closed in 2007 and consolidated into Clewiston.)
2. **Florida Crystals — Okeelanta Mill** (South Bay, Palm Beach) — co-located with a 140 MW biomass cogeneration plant burning bagasse
3. **Florida Crystals — Osceola Mill** (Pahokee, Palm Beach)
4. **Sugar Cane Growers Cooperative of Florida (SCGC) — Bryant Sugar House** (Belle Glade) — 45 grower-owners, ~70,000 acres, ~400,000 tons of raw sugar per year

(That's three operators / four mills — or three mills if you count US Sugar/Florida Crystals/SCGC as the three operators. Both phrasings appear in the trade press.)

**Refining:** Florida Crystals operates a refinery at Yonkers, NY and at Port Everglades; raw sugar from FL/LA is also refined at Domino's Chalmette, LA (St. Bernard Parish) refinery.

## 2. Varieties (Cultivars)

Variety naming follows breeding-station prefixes:

- **Ho** — USDA-ARS Houma, LA (sole release)
- **HoCP** — Joint USDA-ARS Houma + Canal Point release
- **HoL** — Joint Houma + LSU AgCenter release
- **L** — LSU AgCenter Sugar Research Station, St. Gabriel
- **CP** — USDA-ARS Canal Point, FL (joint with University of Florida and the Florida Sugar Cane League)
- **CPCL** — Canal Point + Clewiston (US Sugar) co-release

The two-digit number = year of cross. Example: **HoCP 14-885** = a Houma/Canal Point line from the 2014 crossing season.

| **HoCP 14-885** | 2021 | USDA-ARS Houma + CP | 25–50% | Excellent yield + high sucrose; carries the **Bru1 gene for brown rust resistance**; strong stubbling. Risks: poor cold tolerance, lodging. Shades the row well — natural weed suppression. |

Cold tolerance is a recurring weakness in newer LA varieties — important because hard freezes in late December (post-harvest plant cane is most vulnerable) can damage stubble for next year.

## 3. Crop Cycle

Sugarcane is a perennial grass propagated **vegetatively** from stalk cuttings ("seed cane"). A field is planted once and harvested multiple years (plant cane + several "stubble" / ratoon crops) before being plowed out and replanted, usually after a fallow rotation.

### 3.1 Land prep & rotation

- **Louisiana:** Sugarcane is *traditionally* rotated with **soybeans** or a summer fallow on a typical 4- to 5-year cycle. After the last stubble is harvested in fall, fields are plowed out in spring, soybeans are grown for one season, then fallow / land prep / re-planting begins in late summer. **However, soybean rotation is not universal.** Many LA family operations stay continuous cane (plow out → short fallow → replant) because the economics of a one-year soybean crop have soured — input costs (seed, herbicide, fuel) plus the second harvest's logistics often don't pay back versus going straight back into cane. A meaningful slice of LA acreage is continuous cane in any given year; rotation rates fluctuate with soybean prices. **Treat rotation choice as a per-farm preference, not a default assumption** — the records UI should let a farm declare "no rotation" without making it feel like a missing field.
- **Florida:** Most muck-soil fields are essentially continuous cane. Sand-soil fields rotate more often, sometimes with sweet corn, rice, or fallow.
- **Subsoiling** (deep tillage to break the plow pan) is typical before replanting.

### 3.2 Seed cane & planting

- **Whole-stalk planting** has historically dominated in Louisiana — stalks (~6 ft) laid horizontally in the furrow, two to three stalks deep, then covered with 2–4 inches of soil.
- **Billet planting** (pre-cut chunks) is increasing in Louisiana and is the norm in Florida. It's faster and integrates with chopper-harvester logistics, but requires more seed and clean disease-free seed pieces.
- **Row spacing:** **6 ft rows in Louisiana**; **5 ft rows on muck soils in Florida**, sometimes wider on sand.
- Furrows are 15–18 inches deep with a slight ridge.

### 3.3 Planting calendar

| | Louisiana | Florida |
|---|---|---|
| Plant cane (new planting) | **Late July through October**; peak is August–September | **Late August through January** (much wider window because of mild winter) |
| Plant-cane harvest | Fall of year **after** planting (~14 months after planting) | ~12–14 months after planting |

### 3.4 Plant cane vs. ratoons (stubble crops)

After the plant-cane harvest, the underground rhizomes ("stubble") regrow. Each subsequent crop is a "stubble" or "ratoon" crop:

- **Plant cane** → **1st stubble** → **2nd stubble** → **3rd stubble** → (plow out)

Cycle length:

- **Louisiana:** Historically plant cane + 2 stubbles (3 crops). Modern varieties — particularly **L 01-299** — have pushed many growers to plant cane + 3 stubbles (4 crops). Some now even take a **4th stubble** on top fields.
- **Florida:** Typical is plant cane + 2 stubbles (3 crops); some fields go to 4 crops. Florida's longer growing season + organic muck soils allow good ratoon vigor.

After plow-out, fallow / soybean rotation, then replant.

### 3.5 In-season operations (Louisiana plant cane)

| Month | Operation |
|---|---|
| Aug–Oct | Plant; cover with soil |
| Mar | "Off-bar" cultivation — pulling soil away from the row to expose rhizome and warm the soil |
| Mar–Apr | First fertilizer application |
| Apr–May | Cultivation and additional fertilizer if split |
| Late May | **Layby / closing in** — final cultivation, hill soil back to the row, last fertilizer; field is then "laid by" until harvest |
| Jun–Sep | Growth, scouting, in-season pest/disease control, ripener applications |
| Sep–Jan | Harvest |

Stubble crops skip the planting step but include "stubble shaving" or burning of trash, then off-bar, fertilize, layby.

### 3.6 Fertilization

- **Louisiana:** Total nitrogen typically **80–120 lb N/acre** for plant cane and **100–140 lb N/acre** for stubble. Often split (early spring + layby). P and K applied based on soil test, often ~40–60 lb P₂O₅ and ~80–120 lb K₂O. **Coulter injection** of liquid N directly over the row is increasingly common — combines with fewer tillage passes.
- **Florida muck:** N rates are very low or zero on histosols (organic soils mineralize their own N); **P, K, and silicon (Si)** fertilization are key. Calcium silicate slag is widely applied on muck for Si.
- **Florida sand:** N rates ~150–200 lb N/acre, with K and micronutrients critical.

### 3.7 Weed control

Major herbicides used in LA sugarcane:

- **Atrazine** — broadleaf + winter-grass preemerge; foundational
- **Asulam (Asulox)** — johnsongrass control, applied summer; needs ≥60°F and 20 hours rain-free
- **Terbacil (Sinbar)** — preemerge in plant cane
- **Metribuzin, mesotrione (Callisto), pendimethalin (Prowl)** — common rotation partners
- **2,4-D, dicamba** — broadleaf cleanup
- **Glyphosate** — used as **chemical ripener** (see below) and for fallow burndown (NOT for in-crop weed control — sugarcane is glyphosate-sensitive)
- Resistance pressure on **Italian ryegrass** and **johnsongrass** is the active concern

### 3.8 Ripening

- **Louisiana:** Glyphosate (Roundup PowerMAX 3, 4.8 lb a.e./gal) is the dominant chemical ripener, applied **35–49 days before harvest** (35–42 days is the typical sweet spot). It's labeled for **stubble crops only — not plant cane**. Adds 0.3–0.7 percentage points of sucrose. Some varieties (HoCP 09-804, Ho 12-615, L 14-267) are sensitive — yield can drop. Trinexapac-ethyl (Moddus) is a non-glyphosate alternative.
- **Florida:** Chemical ripening is much less common. Florida's longer growing season + pre-harvest burning + a 6-month grinding window means fields mature naturally across the season. Some glyphosate ripener use exists, especially early-season harvest blocks.

### 3.9 Pre-harvest burning

- **Florida:** **Standard practice.** ~400,000 acres burn each season (Oct–May). Burns remove leaves and tops ("trash"), leaving only the sugar-bearing stalk. Burning is regulated by the Florida Forest Service; permits issued daily based on wind/air-quality conditions. This is a politically charged issue (the "Stop Sugar Field Burning" / Sierra Club / Friends of the Everglades campaigns). A small but growing share of FL acreage is harvested **green** (no burn).
- **Louisiana:** Pre-harvest burning was largely **abandoned around 2000** in favor of green-cane harvesting with chopper harvesters; some post-harvest **trash/residue burning** still occurs on stubble fields to reduce ratoon-stunting and clean up before stubble shaving.

### 3.10 Harvest

- **Mechanical chopper-harvesters** (Case IH Austoft 8000/8800, John Deere CH570/950, Cameco) are universal in both states. Cut the cane, top it, and chop it into ~12 inch billets that fall into a side-tipping wagon ("billet wagon") running alongside.
- Wagons dump into **highway dump trailers** for haul to mill. In FL, larger fields use **double-trailer billet trucks**; in LA, single-trailer dumps are typical.
- Whole-stalk "soldier" harvesters were the standard until ~2005 in LA; now essentially retired for commercial use.
- Cane must reach the mill within **24–48 hours** of cutting before sucrose inversion losses get serious.

### 3.11 Grinding ("zafra" / sugar season)

| | Louisiana | Florida |
|---|---|---|
| Typical start | **Late September / early October** | **Mid-October** |
| Typical end | **Mid-January** | **April / early May** |
| Length | ~3–4 months | ~6–7 months |
| Mill operation | 24/7 once started | 24/7 once started |

Florida runs longer simply because there's more cane (per mill) and the climate allows year-round growth — early-harvest blocks have lower sugar but stretch the campaign.

### 3.12 Final products

- **Raw sugar** — the main product, shipped to refineries (LSR, Domino, Imperial)
- **Blackstrap molasses** — co-product; sold for animal feed, rum distilling, industrial fermentation
- **Bagasse** — fibrous residue; **burned for cogeneration** at every modern mill (Okeelanta's 140 MW plant is the highest-profile example). Net energy exporter at most FL mills.
- **Filter cake / mud** — byproduct used as soil amendment

## 4. Records a Grower Actually Keeps

Per **field**, per **crop year**, growers (and consultants and crop insurance adjusters) want:

**Field setup**
- Field name / number, parish or county, GPS boundary, acreage (planted and harvest-eligible), soil type (e.g., Baldwin silty clay loam in LA; Pahokee muck in FL), drainage / quad
- Variety planted, plant date, seed source (own field, neighbor, certified seed program), seed cane type (whole stalk vs. billet)
- Crop year stage: **plant cane / 1st stubble / 2nd stubble / 3rd stubble / 4th stubble / plowout**

**Operations log (per pass)**
- Date, operation type (plant, fertilize, off-bar, herbicide, insecticide, ripener, harvest, stubble shave, burn)
- Product applied (e.g., "Asulox 3.34F", "Roundup PowerMAX 3", "32% UAN")
- Rate (lb/ac, gal/ac, qt/ac), carrier volume (gal/ac), method (broadcast, band, coulter, aerial, ground)
- Applicator / operator, equipment, weather (temp, wind, RH)
- REI / PHI compliance flags

**Scouting log**
- Date, scout name, observation type (pest, disease, deer/hog damage, lodging, gap, wash-out, weed escape, frost), count or % infested, photo, GPS pin

**Harvest data**
- Cut date(s), tons harvested, **gross tons / net tons** (mill weighs both), **CRS (Commercially Recoverable Sugar) lb per ton**, **TRS (Theoretical Recoverable Sugar)**, **% sucrose** (a.k.a. pol), trash %, mill ticket numbers
- **Yield in tons/acre** and **lb sugar/acre** (the two numbers everyone benchmarks against)
- Premium / discount paid by mill (most LA mills pay on a sugar-content sliding scale)

**Annual / regulatory**
- **FSA-578 acreage report** — required for sugar program participation; reports planted + failed acreage for sugar and seed
- **RMA crop insurance APH** — Approved Actual Production History; lag year — 2024 production reported by the 2026 production-reporting date. Coverage 50–85% of APH yield; example: 75% coverage on a 6,000 lb/ac approved yield = 4,500 lb/ac guarantee
- **Marketing allotment compliance** — processors track this, but growers see it via mill payment

**Disease / pest history (multi-year)**
- RSD test results (every 2–3 years on seed source fields)
- Borer counts (5% infested-stalks treatment threshold)
- Frost / freeze damage events with dates
- Hurricane lodging history

## 5. Pests & Diseases — Scouting Vocabulary

### Insects

| Pest | Latin | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Sugarcane borer** | *Diatraea saccharalis* | Flagship LA pest. Treatment threshold: **5% stalks with larvae feeding in leaf sheaths**. EIL ~6–12% bored internodes (variety-dependent). Scout weekly mid-June through September. |
| **Mexican rice borer** | *Eoreuma loftini* | Newer arrival in LA from TX (first detected ~2008). Pheromone trap scouting; action threshold ~20 moths/trap/week. |
| **Sugarcane aphid** | *Melanaphis sacchari* | First reported on cane in LA in 1999. Honeydew + sooty mold reduces photosynthesis. (Also a major sorghum pest.) |
| **Yellow sugarcane aphid** | *Sipha flava* | Older established pest; less economically important than M. sacchari. |
| **White grubs** | *Phyllophaga, Cyclocephala* spp. | Root feeders; problematic on sand soils and after fallow. |
| **Wireworms** | *Melanotus* spp. | Stand-establishment pest at planting. |
| **Sugarcane beetle** | *Euetheola humilis* | Stalk feeders at base. |
| **West Indian canefly** | *Saccharosydne saccharivora* | Honeydew producer in FL. |

### Diseases

| Disease | Pathogen | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Brown rust** | *Puccinia melanocephala* | Reshaped LA variety mix after LCP 85-384 collapsed. **Bru1 gene** (in HoCP 14-885 and others) confers resistance. |
| **Orange rust** | *Puccinia kuehnii* | Arrived in FL 2007; major selection pressure on FL CP varieties. CP 00-1101 highly susceptible. |
| **Smut** | *Sporisorium scitamineum* | Black "whip" emerges from growing point; major historical disease, suppressed by resistant varieties. |
| **Leaf scald** | *Xanthomonas albilineans* | White "pencil line" on leaves; seed-borne; managed via clean seed program. |
| **Ratoon stunting disease (RSD)** | *Leifsonia xyli* subsp. *xyli* | Xylem-clogging bacterium; can cut yield 12–60%. Spread by harvester blades. Managed by hot-water-treated seed cane and equipment sanitation. |
| **Sugarcane mosaic virus / Sorghum mosaic virus** | SCMV / SrMV | Aphid-vectored; managed by resistant varieties + clean seed. |
| **Yellow leaf** | *Sugarcane yellow leaf virus (SCYLV)* | Aphid-vectored; widespread but variable yield impact. |
| **Red rot** | *Colletotrichum falcatum* | Stalk rot, often follows borer damage. |
| **Pokkah boeng** | *Fusarium* spp. | Sporadic; "twisted top" symptoms. |

### Vertebrates / agronomic / weather

- **Feral hogs** — Louisiana's biggest vertebrate problem; estimated $74M/year statewide ag damage. Rooting destroys plant cane and sets back stubble.
- **Rats** (cotton rat, hispid cotton rat) — chew stalks; major in FL cane.
- **Deer** — minor in cane vs. row crops, but tip damage occurs.
- **Lodging** — wind / hurricane / heavy panicle weight knocks stalks flat; harder to harvest, sucrose loss. Hurricane Francine (Sept 2024) lodged cane in southeast LA.
- **Wash-out / drowning** — flooded furrows kill young cane.
- **Gaps / poor stand** — counted at off-bar; trigger replant decisions.
- **Frost / freeze damage** — late-season LA risk; cane standing into January is exposed; deep freezes (<25°F for >4 hr) damage stubble and start sucrose inversion in standing cane.
- **Trash / residue management** — a records category in itself: burned vs. shredded vs. left as mulch.

A scouting **pin / category enum** for Headland could reasonably look like:

`borer | mex_rice_borer | aphid | white_grub | wireworm | rust_brown | rust_orange | smut | leaf_scald | rsd | mosaic | yellow_leaf | red_rot | hog_damage | rat_damage | deer_damage | lodging | washout | gap | frost | weed_escape | other`

Plus FL-specific: `pre_harvest_burn` (planning / completion record), `green_harvest`.

## 6. Industry Organizations & Research

**Federal research**
- **USDA-ARS Sugarcane Research Unit** — Houma, LA. Variety development (Ho / HoCP series), entomology, plant pathology, soil science.
- **USDA-ARS Sugarcane Field Station** — Canal Point, FL. National crossing site — produces the "true seed" used by both Houma and Texas A&M (Weslaco) breeding programs. Releases CP varieties jointly with University of Florida and the Florida Sugar Cane League.

**State land-grant**
- **LSU AgCenter Sugar Research Station** — St. Gabriel, LA (Iberville Parish). Releases L-series and HoL-series varieties, publishes the annual *Sugarcane Variety Performance* and *Sugarcane Production Handbook*, maintains the variety-advancement dashboard.
- **University of Florida / IFAS — Everglades Research and Education Center (EREC)** — Belle Glade. Variety evaluation, pest management, EDIS publications.

**Industry / grower organizations**
- **American Sugar Cane League (ASCL)** — Thibodaux, LA. The Louisiana grower + processor trade group; funds research at LSU AgCenter and ARS Houma; publishes industry crop summaries.
- **Louisiana Sugar Cane Cooperative / SUGAR (Sugar Growers and Refiners of Louisiana)** — Gramercy, LA. Owns Louisiana Sugar Refining (LSR) refinery jointly with Imperial Sugar; 8 mills + their growers as members.
- **Louisiana Sugarcane Growers Inc.** — separate grower-only org.
- **Florida Sugar Cane League** — Clewiston, FL. Funds CP variety development, lobbies federal sugar program.
- **Sugar Cane Growers Cooperative of Florida (SCGC)** — Belle Glade. 45 grower-owners; ~70,000 acres; operates Bryant Sugar House.
- **American Society of Sugar Cane Technologists (ASSCT)** — annual proceedings (LA + FL divisions); main industry technical journal.

## 7. Economics & Acreage

### 2024/2025 production (USDA NASS)

| | Louisiana | Florida | US total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Harvested acres (sugar + seed) | 520,000 | 417,000 | ~937,000 (mainland cane) |
| Yield (tons/ac) | **31.5** | **42.1** | 37.2 avg |
| Production (million tons cane) | 16.4 | 17.6 | 34.0 |
| Mills operating (2025/26) | 11 | 4 | 15 |
| Tons ground 2024 | 17.36M (record) | ~17.5M | ~35M |
| Industry economic impact | $2.3B | comparable | ~$5B+ |

Florida out-yields Louisiana per acre by ~30–35% — partly variety, partly climate (no winter dormancy), partly muck-soil fertility.

The US accounts for ~3–4% of world raw sugar production; cane sugar is ~55% of US output and beet sugar (Midwest, Pacific NW) is ~45%.

### Federal sugar program (very brief — for marketing pages only)

- **Loan rates:** 19.75¢/lb raw cane sugar, 25.38¢/lb refined beet sugar (FY2020–FY2024). Nonrecourse — processors can forfeit sugar to USDA in lieu of repayment.
- **Marketing allotments:** USDA sets an Overall Allotment Quantity each year (≥85% of estimated US consumption). Allocated by state and processor.
- **Tariff Rate Quotas (TRQs):** ~1.12 million MTRV in-quota raw cane sugar at near-zero tariff; out-of-quota tariff ~15.36¢/lb (effectively prohibitive). 40 countries hold raw cane TRQ allocations; the Dominican Republic, Brazil, Philippines, and Australia are the largest.
- The program is run by **USDA FSA (loans, allotments)** and **USTR + USDA FAS (TRQ administration)**.

### Per-acre economics (LA, illustrative)

- **Production cost** rose from ~$551/ac (2018) to **$858/ac (2023)** per LSU AgCenter — driven by a 130% rise in fertilizer and 82% rise in diesel since 2018. Current full-cost budgets (variable + fixed) are higher still.
- **Revenue** at ~31 tons/ac × ~210 lb sugar/ton × payment in the low-30s¢/lb range (after deductions) → roughly **$1,800–$2,200/ac gross** in a normal year — varies hugely with sugar content and cane price.
- A grower's "extreme sugar" year (Raceland's term) is >250 lb sugar/ton.

### Risk

- **Crop insurance (RMA):** Sugarcane has dedicated crop provisions; APH-based; coverage 50–85%; reported in lb of raw sugar/ac. Late-season hurricane is the biggest single insurable risk in LA; freeze is secondary.
- **Hurricane history** (cited in marketing): Ida (2021), Francine (Sept 2024) lodged significant cane in southeast LA.
